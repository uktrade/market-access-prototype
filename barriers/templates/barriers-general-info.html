{% trans "Find out about trade barriers" %}

When you export goods or services to another country you are protected by rules. They might be the regulations set out by the World Trade Organisation, or by the terms of a free trade agreement between the UK and the country you are exporting to. Most of the time, the rules work well but sometimes there can be problems. The Government works with the World Trade Organisation and other governments to overcome them.

Teams from the Department for International Trade, the Foreign and Commonwealth Office and other departments stand ready to assist UK companies who report trade barriers.

If your problem is being caused by a trade barrier, it will probably fall into one of these categories:

Unfair use of state help or subsidies

Local producers or suppliers receive money or assistance from their government which makes it harder for non-domestic producers and suppliers to compete.

Unreasonable import quotas

Restrictions on what can be sold in a country which go beyond what is allowed by relevant trade agreements or which seem unjustified.

Burdensome customs procedures

Requirements that goods go through checks, or provide documentation, which seems to serve no practical purpose.

Restrictive government procurement rules and practices

Regulations or special requirements which make it difficult or impossible for non domestic suppliers to compete for a contract.

Unreasonable technical regulations, standards and conformity assessment procedures

Regulations or processes which don't seem to serve any legitimate purpose, or which discriminate against importers.

Poor protection of intellectual property rights and geographical indicators

Instances of piracy, counterfeiting or mis-labelling which undermine legitimate export trade. Geographical indicators include Scotch whisky and Stilton cheese.

Unreasonable sanitary and phytosanitary measures

Measures purporting to protect consumer health, animal and plant welfare or the environment which seem unreasonable.

Restrictive rules of origin

Rules requiring products to be able to demonstrate in which countries they were produced, often so that it can be determined whether the good can benefit from preferential access under a bilateral free trade agreement.

Barriers to trade in services and investment, such as unjustified foreign ownership caps, joint venture obligations and discriminatory treatment

Financial rules which make imported services uncompetitive, or unreasonably discourage foreign investment.

Restrictions on other countries' exporters

Policies undertaken by the exporter's government, e.g. to limit exports to a certain country through trade embargo, or to reduce exports to keep domestic prices low.
